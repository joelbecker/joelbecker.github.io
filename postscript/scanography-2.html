Scanography Study Two: Postscript

  • ↓

If you've never tried scan art, I can't recommend it highly enough. It is perhaps the lowest barrier to entry of any form of visual art (or was, until everyone had a camera in their pocket.) It's popular for this reason, and scanography is sometimes seen as an artistic cliché. But I've found it to be a really interesting medium to explore deeply. I love scan art as an artistic processes because it gives rapid feedback, encourages exploration, and creates space for happy accidents. But as a cheeky nod to scan art's reputation, I had originally included the glitched text "everybody's favourite low-effort art form" in the collage cover of this zine. I still think it's a funny joke, but the more I thought about it the more I felt it unintentionally devalued the hours I've spent working with my scanner, the difficulty of tweaking your process to iterate on the result, and the important work of curating the result. In the end, I cut out the words with a craft knife and filled it in with another scan. I don't regret it — creativity thrives in humour, but it's easy to lean on self-deprecation as to deflect real or imagined judgement. And as I've said in other zines and reflections, part of the journey of an artist is staying with your enthusiasms even when your deepest instinct is to hide them away.

Visions of Entropy is part of Stay with Me, CAFKA’s 2023 biennial exhibition of public art.
