Take Nothing, Leave Everything: Postscript

  • ↓

Take Nothing, Leave Everything is by far the most vulnerable piece in this zine collection. I find poetry fundamentally more vulnerable than prose in the same way that I find singing fundamentally more vulnerable than playing an instrument. But the real reason is that these poems are a directly look into my journal at what I've come to see as a formative moment in my life - that's a privilege I've so far given to only a handful of confidants, and it took a lot of courage to publish these for unknown eyes, who might just as easily judge or dismiss as accept and reflect.

I made the difficult decision to let the poems stand on their own, rather than telling the story of writing them. But I'm glad to explore it here in the postscript, where I can also reflect on how writing these poems influenced by creative practice.

I wrote these poems on a solo camping trip I took to escape the city and reconnect with nature. I had just finished setting up camp, and I was about to turn off my phone - to me, a vital part of being in the forest. Without thinking, I moved to take a picture, thinking that I would post a picture to instagram before turning off my phone. But I stopped myself - if I was really here to disconnect, why would I document the experience for the internet? I turned off my phone, took out a blank journal I had brought, and started writing: "take nothing but pictures? even this is far too much...".

This was far from the first time I had created boundaries from technology: but it stands out to me because it was the first time I remember that doing so felt like an act of resistance reclaiming ground in a battle that I was losing. It was also the first time resisting social media had entered my creative practice.

This was partially because I hadn't yet found ways for my creativity to thrive in the city, since leaving behind the contexts of school and summer camp where creative exploration had come naturally to me as a student and camp counsellor. I had joined the corporate workforce a few years prior, I was no longer spending summers in the forest, and social media was becoming a bigger drain on my own attention and the world's.

It wasn't until the pandemic when I started discovering new creative outlets that thrived in my life after camp: my creative habits quite directly emerged out of digital burnout during lockdown. And the more that I develop my creative practice, the more that I look back and see the seeds of it when I chose to leave my Silent Lake campsite in analogue anonymity.

The third poem in this zine is much older: an assignment for a high school writing class that I've remained quite fond of. But it's hard to articulate the strangeness of looking back at myself wax eloquent on the loss of innocent freedom, from I time before I had to pay rent, before I cooked for myself, before I had performance reviews instead of report cards, and before I was losing a battle with the attention merchants for the precious moments of my existence.

But I'm glad that as a teenager I was learning how art could be used to create perspective on my life. And I'm glad that habit stayed with me long enough to survive the journey to Silent Lake, and everywhere it's taken me since.

P.P.S. On the subject of "happy accidents", this is one of the first mixtapes I made in lockdown as a way to escape my phone, both to create the mixtape and to listen to it. The mixtape is based on the driving playlist I made for my trip to Silent Lake (the highlight being Theo Katzman's Country Backroads: "I was driving, driving, driving, and I \ Lost my cell phone"). The collage was a reflection on the trip - "the urban" turned upside down, roadways, waterways, and leaving the city behind. I felt a map would be visually appropriate, as was looking through an old Canadiana magazine when I found this one: a map including the literal route I had driven listening to the mix on the road to Silent Lake. That route is highlighted in red.

I've always loved the serendipity of that mixtape. But it hadn't quite connected until I started this zine how that trip had also led to the taking and leaving poems, and the ripples it continues to have in my life.
